The Web-Based Garage Door Opener

Thanks to the advances in computer technologies, we are now at a point where a computer can be the size of a credit card and cost as much as a dozen cups of coffee. As a result, we can start building computers into our everyday objects. This page outlines the parts needed for such a project: A web-enabled garage door opener.

1. Raspberry Pi 2B

The Raspberry Pi 2B is the brains of the operation. Running its own operating system and connected to the local wi-fi network, this mini-computer will be able to monitor the current status of the garage door (open or closed) and trigger the garage door opener when prompted to do so.

To get it all to work, the Raspberry Pi will run a simple web server, accessible through the local network, that provides a website displaying the status of the garage door and a button to open or close the door.

2. Micro SD Card

No computer is complete without read/write storage capability. On the Raspberry Pi, that storage comes in the form of a Micro SD card. The size of a finger nail, this card can carry both the Raspberry Pi's operating system + a web server application and the custom code neccessary to control the garage door.

3. 2-Channel 5V Relay

The beauty of the Raspberry Pi is it's input/output ports that can be used to control external devices. In this project, these ports will be used to sense the current status of the door and to toggle the garage door opener to open or close the door.

The 2-channel 5V relay is the interface between the Raspberry Pi and the garage door opener itself. It takes a trigger signal from the Rapsberry Pi, converts it to a 5V signal, and sends that to the garaga door opener to trigger it to either open or close the door.

For this project, only one of the two channels on the relay will be used.

4. Magnetic Door Sensor

Having a remote web-enabled garage door opener is more or less meaningless unless you can know, without looking, whether the garage door is open or closed.

To sense the status of the garage door, a magnetic door sensor is used. The sensor consists of a magnet (the thin part) and the sensor itself. Inside the sensor, a circuit is completed when the magnet is close by. In other words, when the cirtuit is closed, the door is closed.

5. Jumper Cables

To connect the Raspberry Pi to the 5V relay and to perform experimentation and beta-testing, jumper cables are required. These cables can connect pins to each other (female-to-female) and make connections inside a breadboard for easy experimentation.

When complete, the project will likely use four female-to-female jumper cables: Two connected to the relay, and two connected to the door sensor.

6. Breadboard

The breadboard is a staple of electronics experimentation. In combination with jumper cables and other electronic units, the breadboard can be used to experiment with different types of circuits in a simple and accessible way before a final circuit and wiring plan is established.

In this project, the breadboard will only be used during the setup phase. Once complete, the breadboard will be replaced by permanent circuits and reused for other projects.
